01Heat kills more people than any other weather event
The WHO calls heat a "silent killer." Most victims never appear in official statistics because heat triggers heart attacks, kidney failure, and respiratory collapse that get recorded under other causes. Researchers estimate the true toll is 2 to 10 times higher than what gets reported.

Heat vs other weather hazards: US annual deaths (10-year avg, 2015 to 2024)

HeatOther weather hazards
In the most recent decade, heat killed more Americans annually than floods, tornadoes, hurricanes, and lightning combined. Even these official figures dramatically undercount heat deaths: the Lancet Countdown estimates 546,000 heat deaths globally per year using excess mortality analysis. 04The hidden crisis: nighttime heat and mental health
Most heat science focuses on afternoon temperatures. But the fastest-rising threat is what happens after dark, and what happens inside people's heads.
100 cities worldwide, 1995 to 2024
Nighttime temps rising up to 10x faster than daytime
A first-of-its-kind study of the world's most dangerous summer air masses found that afternoon highs have barely changed, but nighttime lows are surging. In Mumbai, overnight temperatures during extreme heat are rising 1 degree C every 8 years. Daytime temperatures? 1 degree every 375 years. When nights stay hot, the body cannot recover from daytime heat, and deaths spike.
HERA / Kalkstein et al. 2025: "When Nights Turn Deadly"
Worldwide pattern
83% of cities losing nighttime relief
Of the 100 cities studied, 83% showed a shrinking gap between daytime highs and nighttime lows during the humid, health-threatening air masses that drive most heat deaths. The pattern is strongest in tropical and arid regions (Africa, South Asia, the Middle East), where populations have the least access to air conditioning and healthcare.
HERA / Kalkstein et al. 2025
Global, 2024
Heat and mental health: record impact in 2024
Analysis of over 8 billion geolocated social media posts found that extreme heat worsened expressed emotional wellbeing by a record 132% above baseline in 2024. Beyond sentiment, heat elevates clinical risks of anxiety, depression, substance use disorders, and suicide. The IPCC assigns "very high confidence" that heat adversely affects mental health.
Lancet Countdown 2025, indicator 1.2.5; IPCC AR6 WGII
Sleep and recovery
Record sleep loss from hot nights
Total sleep time lost due to high nighttime temperatures increased 9% in 2024 compared to baseline, the largest increase in a decade. Some locations lost up to 12 additional hours of sleep per person per year. Disrupted sleep compounds cardiovascular risk, impairs cognitive function, and reduces next-day productivity and learning.
Lancet Countdown 2025, indicator 1.1.4
Why this matters for education
Heat impairs learning, memory, and behaviour
Students in unventilated classrooms during heat events show measurably lower test scores. Children who slept badly due to hot nights arrive at school unable to concentrate. Rising eco-anxiety among young people adds a psychological burden on top of the physical one. These are the conditions GFH's Heat Resilience Champions programme is designed to address.
Park et al. 2020, Nature Human Behaviour; Lancet Countdown 2025

About the data behind this dashboard

Every number on this dashboard is traceable to peer-reviewed research, official UN or government agency reports, or recognised global partnerships. All data is current as of March 2026. Here are the sources, grouped by theme.

Heat mortality. The headline figure of 546,000 annual heat deaths comes from the Lancet Countdown 2025 (indicator 1.1.5), which uses a new model framework covering 120 countries and remains the most comprehensive global estimate of heat-related mortality. European summer mortality data (2022 to 2024) comes from an ISGlobal study in Nature Medicine (2025) covering 654 regions across 32 countries. US reported deaths come from JAMA (2024), and US excess mortality estimates from Yale School of Public Health (2025). The US weather hazard comparison uses official NOAA/NWS fatality data (10-year averages, 2015 to 2024). The Europe 93% figure comes from the WMO Atlas of Mortality (1970 to 2019). Note: official heat death counts dramatically undercount the real toll. The Lancet uses excess mortality analysis, which captures the cardiac arrests, kidney failures, and respiratory collapses that heat caused but were never coded as heat deaths.

Workers, productivity, and economics. The ILO Working on a Warmer Planet (2019) provides the $2.4 trillion projection for 2030. The ILO Heat at Work (2024) provides worker exposure data: 2.41 billion workers, 940 million in agriculture, 22.85 million occupational injuries. The Lancet Countdown 2025 provides 640 billion labour hours lost in 2024 and $1.09 trillion in potential income losses. The productivity curve uses Foster et al. (2021), cited via Allianz Research (2025). Forward projections use Zhao et al. in Nature (2024) and Swiss Re Sigma (2024). Callahan and Mankin in Science Advances (2022) provides the inequality angle.

Children and education. The 242 million children disrupted figure comes from UNICEF (2025). The 21% test score decline comes from Park et al. (2020) in Nature Human Behaviour. Future exposure projections use UNICEF's The Coldest Year (2022).

Women and gender. The Arsht-Rockefeller Foundation Scorching Divide report (2023), with analysis by Vivid Economics using CMIP6 climate models, provides the 260% increase in women's heat losses when unpaid work is included, $120 billion in losses across India, Nigeria, and the US, and the projection of 204,000 women's deaths annually by 2050. The methodology PDF (also in our files) details the sWBGT workability functions and Cobb-Douglas production model used. The UN (2025) provides evidence on climate-driven gender-based violence.

Nighttime heat. HERA's "When Nights Turn Deadly" (2025), led by Prof. Laurence Kalkstein, analysed 100 cities worldwide from 1995 to 2024 using the spatial synoptic classification system. It found nighttime temperatures during dangerous air masses rising up to 10x faster than daytime, with 83% of cities losing overnight relief in humid heat events. The Lancet Countdown 2025 (indicator 1.1.4) independently confirms record sleep loss from hot nights in 2024.

Mental health. The Lancet Countdown 2025 (indicator 1.2.5) analysed over 8 billion geolocated social media posts and found heat worsened expressed emotional wellbeing by a record 132% above baseline in 2024. The IPCC AR6 Working Group II assigns "very high confidence" that heat adversely affects mental health, including elevated risks of anxiety, depression, substance use disorders, and suicide.

Proof that interventions work. The Ahmedabad Heat Action Plan is documented in Hess et al. (2018) and AAQR (2022). The LA County indoor temperature ordinance (82 degrees F, passed August 2025, enforcement January 2027) represents emerging regulatory momentum. The Oxford/Smith School study in Nature Sustainability (2026) projects 3.79 billion people in extreme heat by 2050.

Data currency. All metrics use the most recent published data as of March 2026. The Lancet Countdown publishes new figures every October or November. Key sources to check before major presentations: Lancet Countdown annual report, ILO Heat at Work updates, and HERA's ongoing city-level analyses.

What works: evidence-backed solutions
Each intervention below is grounded in peer-reviewed research and real-world implementation. Organised around Globe From Home's workshop programme, drawing on Project Drawdown, Ahmedabad HAP data, and field evidence from partner cities.
01

Personal heat response (first line of defence)

What it is
Training individuals to recognise heat illness symptoms, take protective action (hydration, shade-seeking, modified work schedules), administer basic first aid for heat stroke, and share knowledge with family and community. This is the "first line of defence" that Rajeev identified as foundational to the programme.
Evidence of effectiveness
Heat stroke deaths: 65 to 3
Ahmedabad's Heat Action Plan, which combines early warnings with community education and health worker training, reduced annual heat stroke deaths from 65 (2010) to just 3 (2018).
Hess et al. 2018, Environmental Health Perspectives
Up to 80% fatality if untreated
Heat stroke is a medical emergency. Mortality reaches 80% without prompt cooling and treatment. Basic first response knowledge (recognising symptoms, rapid cooling, calling for help) is the difference between life and death.
Sheraton et al. 2023, BJA Education
70%+ awareness post-outreach
Ahmedabad's community campaigns raised heat illness awareness from under 30% to above 70% in targeted communities.
Knowlton et al. 2014, IIPH-G / Ahmedabad HAP
Expected outcomes from Globe From Home workshops
25 to 40% of participants adopt 3+ protective behaviours within 90 days. Each trained person shares knowledge with an average of 12 additional family and community members (4 household + 8 community), creating a cascade multiplier effect.
02

Cool roofs

What it is
Applying solar-reflective paint or materials to rooftops, particularly in low-income and slum housing. Reflects sunlight rather than absorbing it, reducing indoor temperatures without electricity or air conditioning. One of the most cost-effective heat interventions available.
Evidence of effectiveness
~3 degrees C indoor reduction
Measured in Ahmedabad slum housing after solar reflective white paint application. Pilot covered 3,000+ low-income homes through Mahila Housing Trust.
Vellingiri et al. 2020, Indian J Occup Environ Med
3 to 6 degrees C in field reports
Broader field reports from 20,000+ homes treated with cool roof technology across Ahmedabad slums show indoor reductions of 3 to 6 degrees C depending on roof material.
Mahila Housing Trust / NRDC field data 2024
20 to 40% cooling energy savings
Cool roofs reduce indoor temperatures enough to significantly cut air conditioning demand. In buildings with AC, this translates to measurable energy and emissions savings.
NRDC / Ahmedabad Heat Action Plan 2016
Expected outcomes
Target: 80 m2 of cool roof per trained champion. At a deployment rate of 65% (based on Ahmedabad's community health worker activation rate of 60 to 70%), each cohort of 500 champions creates 26,000 m2 of cool roof surface and avoids approximately 65 tonnes of CO2 per year.
03

Urban gardens and green corridors

What it is
Establishing community gardens, tree canopy, and green corridors in urban heat islands. Vegetation cools through evapotranspiration and shade, while also providing food security, biodiversity, air quality, and community gathering benefits.
Evidence of effectiveness
1.2 degrees C per 10% canopy increase
Ambient temperature reduction for every 10% increase in urban tree canopy coverage.
Ziter et al. 2019, PNAS
2 degrees C corridor cooling
Medellin's 36 green corridors reduced surrounding temperatures by approximately 2 degrees C while connecting fragmented urban ecosystems.
C40 Cities / Medellin Green Corridors programme data
Most funded heat intervention globally
In a review of over 200 World Bank urban projects, green features (parks, trees, green spaces) were the single most common type of heat intervention, appearing in 48% of all heat-related projects. This makes greening the most bankable and scalable heat solution.
World Bank, Combating Heat in Cities 2024
Expected outcomes
Target: 22 community garden sites per pilot city. Expected 2.1 degrees C ambient cooling in surrounding area. Co-benefits include local food production, improved air quality, and community social cohesion.
04

School heat mapping and adaptation

What it is
Mapping heat exposure in and around schools to identify the hottest classrooms, routes, and outdoor areas. Using this data to drive adaptations: shade structures, schedule changes, hydration stations, and heat-aware teaching practices. Directly addresses the 33,000 school closures seen in Bangladesh and the 21% learning outcome decline documented in heat-exposed students.
Evidence of effectiveness
Cumulative learning loss from heat
Each additional hot school day (above 32 degrees C) measurably reduces test scores. The effect compounds over the school year and disproportionately affects students without access to cooling.
Park et al. 2020, Nature Human Behaviour
559 million children exposed
To high heatwave frequency globally. Many in the Global South walk to school in extreme heat without shade or cooling infrastructure.
UNICEF, The Coldest Year 2022
242 million students disrupted
In 85 countries during 2024 alone. Heatwaves were the most common climate hazard causing school closures, affecting 118 million students in April alone.
UNICEF, Learning Interrupted 2025
Expected outcomes
Target: 35 schools per pilot city mapped and adapted. Expected 15 to 17% attendance improvement during heat season. Heat mapping data becomes a reusable asset for city planners and education departments.
05

Solar-powered fans for schools

What it is
Installing solar-powered ventilation and cooling fans in classrooms. Provides immediate thermal relief without grid dependency or electricity cost, making it sustainable for schools in off-grid or energy-poor areas. Pairs with cool roofs and shade structures for compound cooling effect.
Evidence of effectiveness
Global cooling demand tripling by 2050
Energy demand for space cooling is projected to triple by mid-century, mostly in developing countries. Solar-powered alternatives bypass grid dependency and recurring energy costs entirely.
IEA, The Future of Cooling 2018
Air-conditioned schools offset 80% of heat's learning impact
Park et al. found that schools with air conditioning experienced roughly one-fifth of the learning loss compared to schools without any cooling. Even basic ventilation (fans, airflow) provides meaningful improvement.
Park et al. 2020, Nature Human Behaviour
Expected outcomes
Target: 35 schools equipped per pilot city. Combined with cool roofs and schedule adaptation, expected to maintain classroom temperatures within tolerable learning range during 90%+ of school hours in heat season.
06

Advocacy campaigns

What it is
Training community leaders, parents, teachers, and local officials to advocate for heat-protective policies: workplace temperature limits, school schedule adjustments, public cooling infrastructure, and heat action plan adoption. This is how individual awareness becomes systemic change.
Evidence of effectiveness
23 states, 130+ cities
Following Ahmedabad's model, India's NDMA is now working with 23 heat-prone states and over 130 cities and districts to develop and implement heat action plans. Community demand and political pressure drove this national scale-up.
Padmanaban 2021, Eos / AGU; NRDC / IIPHG India programme
33 founding cities
C40 launched the Cool Cities Accelerator in November 2025 with 33 founding cities and backing from Rockefeller Foundation. Growing political will means advocacy has receptive audiences.
C40 Cool Cities Accelerator 2025
Most heat laws are too general
In most countries, existing workplace heat provisions fail to address the specific dangers workers face. Targeted advocacy for enforceable temperature limits, mandatory rest periods, and hydration access is the gap.
ILO, Heat at Work 2024
Expected outcomes
Target: 600 community advocates trained per pilot city. Expected to drive at least 1 local policy change per city within 12 months (workplace breaks, school schedule adjustments, or public cooling infrastructure). 28% reduction in heat symptoms in communities with active advocacy programmes.

01Why Phoenix, Arizona
Deadliest US city for heat
645 heat deaths in Maricopa County (2023)
Phoenix recorded the highest heat-related death toll of any US county. The city experienced 31 consecutive days above 43 degrees C (110°F) in summer 2023.
Maricopa County Dept. of Public Health 2024
Urban heat island
Up to 5.5°C hotter than surroundings
Phoenix's sprawling, low-albedo urban footprint creates one of the most intense urban heat islands in the world. Night-time temperatures no longer cool enough for recovery.
Arizona State University, Urban Climate Research Center
Funder relevance
US-based data for US funders
Many target funders are US-based. A Phoenix pilot provides domestic proof-of-concept alongside international programmes, demonstrating that heat resilience education is needed everywhere.
02Target areas
Focus on the communities within the Phoenix metro area with the highest heat vulnerability and lowest access to cooling.
🏙️

South Phoenix

Lowest tree canopy coverage in the metro. Highest concentration of outdoor workers and low-income households. Existing community organisations to partner with.
🏙️

Mesa

Third-largest city in Arizona. Large school district. Significant agricultural worker population exposed to extreme heat during harvest seasons.
🏙️

Central Phoenix

Highest density of unsheltered population. Heat is the leading cause of death among unhoused residents. Cooling centre activation a key intervention.
03Scale projections

Projected reach over 3 years

Urban pilot model: concentrated in one metro area with expansion to surrounding counties.
Year 1
2,000 learners across 3 districts
School partnerships and community outreach in South Phoenix and Mesa. Baseline data collection.
Year 2
8,000 learners across Maricopa County
County-wide expansion. Integration with Maricopa County heat relief network and cooling centres.
Year 3
20,000 learners across Arizona
State-level partnerships. Model available for replication in Las Vegas, Houston, and other heat-vulnerable US cities.

01Why Kerala
Humid heat danger
Wet-bulb temperatures approaching survivability limits
Kerala's combination of high heat and high humidity creates wet-bulb conditions that are more dangerous than dry heat alone. The human body cannot cool itself through sweating when wet-bulb temperature exceeds 35 degrees C.
Lancet Countdown 2025; Raymond et al. 2020, Science Advances
Compound climate risk
Floods + heat in alternating seasons
Kerala experienced catastrophic floods in 2018 and 2019, followed by record heat. Communities face compounding climate shocks with diminishing recovery windows between events.
India Meteorological Department / Kerala SDMA
Education infrastructure
96%+ literacy rate
Kerala has India's strongest education system and highest civic engagement. This makes it an ideal environment for education-driven climate adaptation with high adoption potential.
Census of India / Kerala Planning Board
02Target regions
Focus on districts with the highest compound climate risk and strongest institutional partners.
🏙️

Thiruvananthapuram

State capital. High population density. Coastal humidity amplifies heat stress. Strong government and research infrastructure for pilot coordination.
🏙️

Kochi

Major port city with dense informal labour sector. Extreme humidity during pre-monsoon season. Smart City Mission participant with digital infrastructure.
🏙️

Kozhikode

Northern Kerala's largest city. Agricultural hinterland with outdoor worker exposure. Strong community-based organisation network for programme delivery.
03Scale projections

Projected reach over 3 years

Humid heat pilot: testing interventions adapted for high-humidity environments.
Year 1
3,000 learners across 3 districts
Adapting workshop content for humid heat contexts. Partnering with Kerala SDMA and local schools.
Year 2
10,000 learners across 7 districts
District-level expansion. Integration with Kerala's existing disaster management and education networks.
Year 3
25,000 learners state-wide
Full state coverage. Model becomes reference for humid heat adaptation across South and Southeast Asia.

01Why small island nations
Existential climate threat
1.5°C is a survival threshold
For small island developing states, climate change is not a future risk but a present emergency. Rising seas, intensifying heat, and coral bleaching threaten entire national economies and habitability.
IPCC AR6 WGII, Chapter 15: Small Islands
Compound vulnerability
Sea level rise + heat + storms
Island communities face overlapping climate risks with very limited infrastructure, healthcare capacity, and fiscal space to respond. Heat compounds every other stressor.
UNEP, Global Adaptation Gap Report 2024
Tourism-dependent economies
30 to 80% of GDP from tourism
Heat and climate impacts directly threaten the economic engine of most small island nations. Building community resilience protects both lives and livelihoods.
World Bank, SIDS Development Data
02Target regions
Focus on island nations with strong government engagement on climate, existing international partnerships, and scalable school systems.
🏝️

Fiji

COP23 presidency. Strong climate policy leadership. 900,000 population with centralised education system. Existing partnerships with UNDP and Pacific Islands Forum.
🏝️

Maldives

Global symbol of climate vulnerability. Extreme heat and humidity. 500,000 population. Government actively seeking climate adaptation partnerships.
🏝️

Tuvalu

One of the most climate-vulnerable nations on earth. 11,000 population allows for whole-nation pilot. Digital statehood initiative provides unique partnership opportunity.
03Scale projections

Projected reach over 3 years

Island model: smaller populations but whole-nation coverage possible. High visibility for global advocacy.
Year 1
1,500 learners across 1 nation
Single-country deep pilot (likely Fiji). Full school system integration and community training.
Year 2
5,000 learners across 3 nations
Expansion to Maldives and Tuvalu. Adaptation of content for island-specific heat and humidity contexts.
Year 3
15,000 learners across 6 nations
Pacific and Indian Ocean coverage. Model presented at UNFCCC and Pacific Islands Forum as replicable framework.
